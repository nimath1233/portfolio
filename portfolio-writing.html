Portfolio Writing & Personal Branding

Interactive portfolio design and personal branding session

Honestly, I used to think portfolios were only for designers, photographers, or artists — people who needed to display visual work. I never once thought that someone studying IT would need one. That assumption was completely wrong, and this session was what made me realise it.

When the session started, I was surprised by how much goes into building a portfolio the right way. It is not just about collecting files and putting them in a folder. It is about choosing the right pieces of work, presenting them clearly, and telling the story behind each one. I had never thought about my own work that way before.

By the end, I understood that in IT, a portfolio can do something a CV simply cannot — it shows employers what you have actually done, not just what you claim you can do. That difference is huge, and it motivated me to start building my own straight away.

💡 Special Notice: This very e-portfolio website is the result of what I learned in this session.

📁What is a Portfolio & Why Build One?

During this session, I learned that a Portfolio is a curated collection of materials that showcases my skills, projects, achievements, and academic deliverables. Especially in IT, I realized a portfolio is a critical tool for my career:

Proves My Capability

I learned that while a CV only claims skills, my portfolio can prove them by displaying my code, screenshots, and live deployments.

Career Advancement

The lecturer showed us how a portfolio acts as a personal brand, helping me stand out to recruiters and secure internships.

Professional Development

I realized that organizing my work forces me to reflect on my learning, identify gaps, and document my achievements.

Reflective Record

I learned this will serve as a historical record of my growth, projects completed, and feedback received throughout my studies.

📂Types of Portfolios

I learned that portfolios serve different purposes and target different audiences. The four main types include:

  • Personal Portfolio: Curated for my self-reflection and documenting my personal milestones. Often private.
  • Career / Dossier Portfolio: My complete archive of professional experience, qualifications, and certificates.
  • Employment Portfolio: My targeted, public showcase of my best projects and skills customized for job applications.
  • Assessment Portfolio: Specifically created to demonstrate that I have met the learning outcomes of an academic module. This site is my assessment portfolio.

📋15-Step Guide to Building a Portfolio

I learned these 15 key steps to design, develop, and maintain my high-quality portfolio:

  1. 1
    Define the Purpose: I must know what I want to achieve (e.g., pass this academic module or get an internship).
  2. 2
    Identify the Audience: I will align my layout and tone with what recruiters or instructors expect.
  3. 3
    Choose the Format: I decided on a custom HTML/CSS site rather than drag-and-drop platforms.
  4. 4
    Curate My Best Work: I need to show projects I can explain in detail, prioritizing quality over quantity.
  5. 5
    Organize Logically: I will use clear categories (e.g., CV, Ethics, Dining, Interview) and navigation.
  6. 6
    Add Reflections: I must explain what I learned, challenges I faced, and what I would improve next time.
  7. 7
    Uphold Design Consistency: I will use a harmonious color scheme, clean fonts, and uniform margins.
  8. 8
    Optimize for Mobile: I ensure my site is responsive and renders beautifully on smartphones.
  9. 9
    Maintain Privacy: I must avoid publishing database keys, credentials, or sensitive client info.
  10. 10
    Include Contact Info: I will link my email and LinkedIn so readers can contact me.
  11. 11
    Update Regularly: I will add new deliverables, projects, and certifications as I complete them.
  12. 12
    Seek Feedback: I will share drafts with peers and mentors to identify typos or confusing sections.
  13. 13
    Keep Backups: I will use Git to back up my code and assets and prevent loss of work.
  14. 14
    Proofread Content: I will check spelling and grammar multiple times to ensure professionalism.
  15. 15
    Ensure Accessibility: I will use descriptive alt tags for images, clean headings, and high contrast.

🌐Online Branding & Word Doc Context

I learned that building my digital presence requires selecting the right tools and presenting my skills in the proper context:

🚀 Top Portfolio Sources I Can Use

✔ LinkedIn: Standard for professional profiles and networks.
✔ Weebly / Wix: Easy drag-and-drop website builders.
✔ GitHub Pages: Perfect for hosting static project code.

✍️ Portfolio Writing Tips I Must Remember

✘ Copying descriptions directly without writing my own reflection.
✘ Overcomplicating the design with flash animations or clashing colors.
✘ Forgetting to provide links to my live projects or code repositories.

Writing Context: I learned that a good portfolio document should outline what the project is, the problem solved, technology stack used, my contribution, a diagram or mockup, code snippets, and a final reflection. I realized this gives the employer a complete view of my analytical and technical capabilities.

My Reflection

Before this session, I thought portfolios were only for artists or designers, not IT students. I didn't think I had anything to show! But building this e-portfolio helped me see that my work is worth sharing. It gave me a place to bring together everything I've learned in these lectures and reflections.

Creating this site was a lot of work, but seeing it finished makes me really proud. It's not just a list of my modules; it's a real proof of my skills and growth. It makes me feel much more prepared to show my projects to future employers.

"A CV tells people what you can do. A portfolio proves it. Your portfolio is not just a collection of work — it is proof of your promises."
